## Research Question

* Does the frequency of physical fighting at school significantly affect a student's BMI?
* Does a student's gender interact with this relationship?

## Variables

* `PhysicalFightingAtSchool`: categorical explanatory variable representing fighting frequency (coded from 1 to 7).
* `WhatIsYourSex`: categorical explanatory variable representing gender (Male / Female).
* `BMI`: quantitative response variable measuring Body Mass Index, calculated from weight and height.

## Method

Two-Way ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) was used to compare mean BMI across different physical fighting frequencies and genders, and to test for a significant interaction effect between the two explanatory variables.

## Main Results

After data cleaning, the Two-Way ANOVA found a statistically significant main effect for fighting frequency (p = 0.0029 < 0.05) and gender (p < 0.001) on BMI. However, the interaction effect between fighting frequency and gender was NOT statistically significant (p = 0.0813 > 0.05). 

## Conclusion

Both gender and the frequency of school fights have a significant independent relationship with adolescent BMI. The lack of a significant interaction effect is a crucial finding. It indicates that the relationship between body size and school conflicts follows a similar pattern for both male and female students, highlighting a universal social-physical dynamic in schools rather than a gender-specific issue. This conclusion should be stated cautiously because the data show association, not causation.
